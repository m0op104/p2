# Google UX Design Professional Certificate

(Dec 2025) - Finished just before the end of 2025, this course featured updated modules that removed the need for peer-graded assignments, a welcome change given the prevalence of cheating. There were many uploaded incomplete assignments and reviewing them is a total waste of time.

My goal was to understand the UX development cycle, which covers seven key areas:

* Foundations of User Experience (UX) Design
* Start the UX Design Process: Empathize, Define, and Ideate
* Build Wireframes and Low-Fidelity Prototypes
* Conduct UX Research and Test Early Concepts
* Create High-Fidelity Designs and Prototypes in Figma
* Build Dynamic User Interfaces (UI) for Websites
* Design a User Experience for Social Good & Prepare for Jobs

In practice, the process is:

* Discover and understand user needs.
* Translate the general idea into clear statements. For example: *Mary is a housewife who sometimes needs to order pizza, but she finds there is no app for her favourite store—or the existing app is difficult to use due to reasons A, B, and C.*
* Brainstorm ideas on paper: sketch five different layouts for each screen, then combine the best elements.
* Ask for feedback on these paper prototypes.
* Convert the paper screens into digital designs in Figma, adding visual elements.
* Use Figma's interactive features to build a demo and gather feedback again.
* Deliver the final design to developers, including the colour scheme, motion flow, and design rationale.

# MoMA Modern and Contemporary Art and Design 
(Jan 2026) - I picked this course because it fit neatly into a one‑month Coursera Plus subscription. In reality, it only took me three weeks, I got sick for the remaining week. The course included four modules: 

* Modern Art & Ideas
* Seeing Through Photographs
* What Is Contemporary Art?
* Fashion as Design

Overall, it was very helpful because it provided a clear guideline on how to look at an artwork. The Fashion as Design module left me uncertain—whether fashion can truly be called “art”—but it did introduce many aspects of modern Western clothing development. The only drawback was that the course contained many leftist political elements.

Anyway, it inspired me to think differently: an art piece is essentially a story the artist wants to tell. We can evaluate their storytelling skill separately, but we don’t necessarily have to agree with the story itself.

I tried to search for a badge for the MoMA course, and it seems there used to be one. But why did they remove it?
